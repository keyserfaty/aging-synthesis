Sulforaphane (Broccoli Sprout Extract)

Sulforaphane (Broccoli Sprout Extract)

Verdict: Suggestive Last reviewed: 2026-04-25 Triangulated against anchor: NMN (Suggestive)

TL;DR

Sulforaphane is the cleanest natural Nrf2 activator in human research, with consistent biomarker effects on antioxidant gene expression and modest signals on cardiometabolic and detoxification markers. Mechanism is established; longevity-specific evidence is thin. Verdict: Suggestive — interesting biology, ongoing translational research, no hard-endpoint longevity evidence yet.

What it is

An isothiocyanate generated when glucoraphanin (abundant in broccoli, especially broccoli sprouts) is hydrolyzed by myrosinase. Sold as broccoli sprout extract or sulforaphane-stabilized supplements. Doses range broadly; trials often use ~30-100 mg sulforaphane equivalent / day.

Proposed mechanism

  • Activation of Nrf2 transcription factor → upregulation of Phase II detoxification enzymes (NQO1, GST, HO-1) → improved antioxidant capacity
  • Anti-inflammatory effects via NF-κB modulation
  • Possible epigenetic effects (HDAC inhibition at high concentrations)

Confidence: Established for the Nrf2 pathway; Plausible for downstream aging-relevant effects.

Evidence ladder

Invertebrate / animal (T4-T5)

Healthspan benefits in rodent disease models. ITP not tested. Lifespan single-lab.

Human (T2)

  • Cardiometabolic markers — small RCTs show improvements in lipids, blood pressure, glycemic markers; effect sizes modest.
  • Detoxification biomarkers — sulforaphane reliably induces Phase II enzymes; this is mechanistically informative but not directly clinical.
  • Autism RCT (Singh 2014, PNAS) — improved behavioral measures; controversial replication.
  • Cancer chemoprevention — biomarker trials in bladder, breast, prostate populations show signals on detoxification markers; hard-endpoint cancer prevention trials lacking.
  • No mortality or aging-endpoint RCT.

Confounds

  • Bioavailability variability — myrosinase activity (gut microbial or supplement-added) varies enormously across populations and products.
  • Standardization — sulforaphane content of supplements varies substantially across products.
  • Glucoraphanin vs sulforaphane — pre-hydrolyzed vs non-hydrolyzed products produce different bioavailability profiles.

Conflict of interest scan

  • Some commercial broccoli-sprout-extract manufacturers sponsor trials.
  • Independent academic interest is real (Talalay group at Hopkins is foundational).
  • Net: modest discount on commercial claims.

Human translation

Honest read: Sulforaphane is the best-evidenced natural Nrf2 activator and produces real biochemical effects in humans at achievable doses. Whether those biochemical effects translate to longevity is unclear; the mechanistic premise is more solid than for many other polyphenols. As a dietary practice (eat broccoli sprouts), it's safe and cheap; as a longevity supplement, the case is mechanism-driven rather than outcome-driven.

Calibrated verdict

Suggestive. Better mechanistic foundation than most polyphenols; comparable hard-endpoint evidence (i.e., none). Sits at the same band as NMN.

Compared to NMN (Suggestive), sulforaphane has cleaner mechanism in humans (Nrf2 activation is reliably demonstrated) but less mouse lifespan data and similar lack of human aging endpoints.

Compared to curcumin (Mostly hype), sulforaphane has better in-human bioavailability and cleaner mechanism activation; one band above curcumin.

Confidence interval on verdict

  • Could move to Probable with positive ITP test or human aging-endpoint RCT — neither imminent.
  • Could move to Mostly hype if the cardiometabolic signal fails to consolidate in larger trials.
  • Most likely 2-year trajectory: stable.

Open questions

  • Q: What is the optimal supplement form (broccoli sprout, glucoraphanin + myrosinase, stabilized sulforaphane) for reliable in-vivo Nrf2 activation?
  • Q: Does Nrf2 activation in humans translate to outcomes that matter (mortality, age-related disease incidence) or only to surrogates?
  • Q: Are there subpopulations where Nrf2 activation is harmful (some cancer contexts where Nrf2 promotes tumor survival)?
