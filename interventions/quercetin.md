# Quercetin (Standalone, Distinct from D+Q Senolytic Combo)

**Verdict:** Mostly hype (as standalone longevity supplement) / Suggestive (as part of senolytic D+Q combination, see senolytics page)
**Last reviewed:** 2026-04-25
**Triangulated against anchor:** Resveratrol (Mostly hype) — similar polyphenol profile

## TL;DR

Quercetin is a popular flavonoid supplement with weak standalone longevity evidence. Its strongest case is as a **partner in the dasatinib + quercetin (D+Q) senolytic combination** — see `interventions/senolytics-dq-fisetin.md`. As a solo supplement, evidence is dominated by small biomarker trials, mixed observational data on flavonoid-rich diets, and considerable industry-driven literature. Verdict: **Mostly hype** for the standalone longevity supplement framing.

## What it is

A flavonol abundant in onions, capers, apples, berries. Sold as supplement at 500-1000 mg/day. Bioavailability is poor (similar to other polyphenols) but better than curcumin. Aglycone vs glycoside forms differ in absorption.

## Proposed mechanism

- Antioxidant / anti-inflammatory in vitro
- Mast cell stabilization (allergy applications)
- AMPK activation
- Senolytic activity (in combination with dasatinib particularly)
- Possible effects on Bcl-2 family proteins (apoptosis regulation in senescent cells)

**Confidence: Established for individual biochemistry; Plausible for senolytic activity in combination contexts; Hypothetical for standalone aging benefit at oral supplement doses.**

## Evidence ladder

### Invertebrate (T5)

Lifespan extension in C. elegans reported. Modest effects.

### Mouse / rat (T4)

- Healthspan effects in disease models.
- **As part of D+Q senolytic combination:** strong replicated mouse healthspan data (see senolytics page).
- **As standalone:** thinner evidence; ITP not formally tested.

### Human (T2)

- **D+Q senolytic trials** — covered separately under senolytics; quercetin is a component.
- **Standalone quercetin RCTs** — mixed, mostly small, on cardiometabolic markers, blood pressure, allergy symptoms, exercise performance. Effect sizes generally modest.
- **Observational dietary flavonoid intake** associates with modestly lower CV mortality across multiple cohorts; quercetin intake is one component.
- **No mortality or aging-endpoint RCT** on standalone quercetin.

## Confounds

- **Bioavailability** problems familiar from other polyphenols.
- **Combination effects** (D+Q) likely reflect synergy, not quercetin alone.
- **Dietary observational data** confounded by overall diet quality.
- **Industry-driven supplement literature** is large.

## Conflict of interest scan

- Heavy supplement industry presence.
- Most independent academic interest is in the D+Q combination, not standalone quercetin for aging.

## Human translation

Honest read: Standalone quercetin supplementation has weak evidence for longevity. The interesting biology (senolytic) requires the dasatinib partner; quercetin alone does not robustly engage that pathway. For specific clinical applications (allergy, mast cell disorders), modest evidence supports use; for "longevity," the case is essentially extrapolation from in vitro and combination-trial data.

## Calibrated verdict

**Mostly hype** for standalone longevity use. The senolytic case for quercetin lives in the D+Q page; readers interested in the senolytic application should reference that page.

Compared to **resveratrol (Mostly hype)**, quercetin has marginally stronger biology (cleaner senolytic mechanism in combination) but a similar bioavailability ceiling and lack of standalone hard-endpoint evidence.

## Confidence interval on verdict

- Stable. Standalone quercetin is unlikely to gain a strong evidence base separately from its combination uses.

## Open questions

- Q: At what dose / formulation does quercetin alone reliably trigger senescent-cell apoptosis without dasatinib? In vitro suggests very high doses; in vivo standalone effects are weak.
- Q: Are there sub-populations where standalone quercetin provides benefit (mast cell disorders, specific allergy phenotypes)?
