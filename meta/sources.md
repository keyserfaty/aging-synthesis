# Trusted Sources

Where the synthesis pulls evidence from, and why each is trusted (or trusted with caveats).

---

## Primary literature aggregators

- **PubMed** — gold standard for biomedical literature search. Use for systematic searches.
- **Europe PMC** — broader coverage, full-text search, includes pre-prints.
- **bioRxiv / medRxiv** — pre-prints. Tier-discounted (treated as one tier below the published equivalent until peer-reviewed).
- **ClinicalTrials.gov** — pre-registration status, results posting (variable quality).

## Aging-specific databases

- **Interventions Testing Program (ITP)** — NIA-funded mouse lifespan testing across 3 sites with strict standardization. The single most authoritative source on whether a drug extends mouse lifespan. Results never tier-discounted.
- **DrugAge** — curated database of pharmacological lifespan-extending interventions across species. Useful as a starting list; entries should still be verified against primary literature.
- **GenAge / Open Genes** — genetic / mechanistic data, less directly used.
- **Aging Cell, GeroScience, Aging (Albany NY)** — primary journals.
- **NIH RP2 (Rejuvenation Project, partial replication)** — emerging effort to replicate lifespan results; use as supplementary evidence to ITP.

## Calibration authorities

These are people whose published positions I triangulate against when methodology is silent. Not infallible — but their reasoning is documented and contestable.

- **Matt Kaeberlein** — long-form podcast/blog/Twitter critiques; his lab does longitudinal aging studies (dog aging project). Generally skeptical, well-calibrated.
- **The ITP team** (Strong, Harrison, Miller and successors) — published rationales for each intervention's testing and outcomes.
- **Brad Stanfield (MD)** — public reviews, especially on supplements; usually well-cited though not always neutral.
- **Cochrane reviewers** — when a Cochrane systematic review exists on an intervention, prefer it over individual studies.
- **Peter Attia** — included with caveat (commercial conflicts; some claims outrun evidence). Useful for surfacing what's popular but should not be a calibration authority for verdicts.
- **Aubrey de Grey** — included with caveat (longevity advocate; specific framing). Useful historical perspective.

## Sources we explicitly distrust as primary evidence

- Supplement-industry-affiliated review papers without disclosure
- "Functional medicine" textbooks
- Single-source blog posts citing themselves
- Podcast claims unsupported by published data

These can be useful as signals of *what the public believes*, which is itself relevant to "public confusion" prioritization. But they are never elevated to evidence.

## Paywall handling

If a paper is paywalled and unobtainable, the page notes "behind paywall — abstract-only synthesis" and we are honest about the limit. Pre-prints and alternate access are searched for first.
